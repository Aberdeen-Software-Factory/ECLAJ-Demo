About the project

This platform serves as the digital hub for the first comprehensive empirical analysis of private antitrust enforcement and access to justice within the UK Competition Appeal Tribunal (CAT). The project examines a wide range of variables, including case duration, claim types, and the parties involved in litigation related to competition damages claims (Section 47A) and collective actions (Section 47B). Its aims are twofold: firstly, to map litigation trends and identify the main characteristics of competition law cases in the UK; secondly, to assess whether access to justice is genuinely upheld through this system.

While competition law is theoretically designed to protect markets and consumers from anti-competitive practices, the reality of litigating these claims is often complicated by enforcement complexities and hidden costs. This project systematically analyses over twenty years of judicial data to answer a vital question: Does the current legal framework effectively serve those harmed by monopolistic behaviour and cartels?

By going beyond doctrinal legal analysis and employing data science and empirical methods, this research seeks to provide clear insights into these issues.

Principal Investigator

Dr Claudio Lombardi University Profile

Dr. Lombardi's research focuses on the intersection of competition law and empirical legal studies.

This project bridges the gap between theoretical antitrust frameworks and the financial realities of litigation, providing policymakers, practitioners, and scholars with open-access, data-driven insights into the functioning of the UK's primary competition court.
